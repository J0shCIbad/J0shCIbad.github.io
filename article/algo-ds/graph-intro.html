Graph Algorithms

Introduction

Introduction:

Graph and network algorithms constitute an essential category of algorithms upon which a great many modern technologies depend on. With graphs and networks being the primary data structure used to represent relationships and connections between entities, they are used to provide computational abstractions to concrete objects like circuits, routes, computer networks, artificial neural networks, and even sets of tasks.

The algorithms designed to operate on these data structures determine how accurately and efficiently machines are able to route vehicles, handle logistics, plan or schedule tasks, and establish optimal assignment of workers to positions. Even the internet itself depends on graph algorithms, be it for TCP/IP routing protocols which facilitates network communication, or for assigning clients to internet service provider servers to accomodate the vast number of users needing to establish connection to the internet.

What is a Graph?

A graph is a set of entities known as vertices or nodes connected by a set of lines called edges. These sets may be denoted by sets V and E respectively, and the graph may be defined as graph G = (V, E).

A vertex v is adjacent to a vertex w if they are connected by an edge e. Both vertices v and w are said to be incident to edge e by which they are connected. A vertex's neighbours are all the vertices adjacent to it, while its degree is the number of neighbours that it has. In a graph where edges have direction (directed graph) a vertex's out-degree is defined as the quantity of edges flowing away from the vertex to it's neighbours, while it's in-degree is the quantity of edges flowing towards the vertex from its neighbours. The vertex's degree is a sum of its out-degree and in-degree.

An edge e is represented by the pair of vertices it is incident on (v, w). In an undirected graph, edges have no direction and the ordering of the pair is insignificant. However, in a directed graph, the edges, known as directed edges, have direction making the ordering of the vertex pair important. An edge may or may not be weighted, that is, to have an associated weight or cost ce . The graphs containing these edges may also be descibed as weighted or unweighted.

An edge incident on the same vertex is called a loop and a vertex may share multiple edges with a neighbouring vertex. A graph is said to be a simple graph if it is unweighted, undirected, has no loops, and has no multiple edges. A simple graph allowing multiple edges is called a multigraph . Most of the time, the term "graph" denotes a simple graph unless otherwise stated.
