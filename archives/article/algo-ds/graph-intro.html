Graph Algorithms

Introduction

Introduction:

Graph and network algorithms constitute an essential category of algorithms upon which a great many modern technologies depend on. With graphs and networks being the primary data structure used to represent relationships and connections between entities, they are used to provide computational abstractions to concrete objects like circuits, routes, computer networks, artificial neural networks, and even sets of tasks.

The algorithms designed to operate on these data structures determine how accurately and efficiently machines are able to route vehicles, handle logistics, plan or schedule tasks, and establish optimal assignment of workers to positions. Even the internet itself depends on graph algorithms, be it for TCP/IP routing protocols which facilitates network communication, or for assigning clients to internet service provider servers to accomodate the vast number of users needing to establish connection to the internet.

What is a Graph?

A graph is a set of entities known as vertices or nodes connected by a set of lines called edges (also known as links or arcs). These sets may be denoted by sets V and E respectively, and the graph may be defined as graph G = (V, E).

Components of a Graph

A vertex v is adjacent to a vertex w if they are connected by an edge e. Both vertices v and w are said to be incident to the edge e by which they are connected. A vertex's neighbours are all the vertices adjacent to it, while its degree is the number of neighbours that it has. In a graph where edges have direction (directed graph) a vertex's out-degree is defined as the quantity of edges flowing away from the vertex to it's neighbours, while it's in-degree is the quantity of edges flowing towards the vertex from its neighbours. The vertex's degree in this case is the sum of its out-degree and in-degree.

An edge e is represented by the pair of vertices it is incident on (v, w). In an undirected graph, edges have no direction and the ordering of the pair is insignificant. However, in a directed graph, the edges, known as directed edges, have direction making the ordering of the vertex pair important. An edge may or may not be weighted, that is, to have an associated weight or cost ce . The graphs containing these edges may also be descibed as weighted or unweighted.

An edge incident on the same vertex is called a loop and a vertex may share multiple edges with a neighbouring vertex. A graph is said to be a simple graph if it is unweighted, undirected, has no loops, and has no multiple edges. A simple graph allowing multiple edges is called a multigraph . Most of the time, the term "graph" denotes a simple graph unless otherwise stated.

Walks and Circuits

A walk is a series of edges joining a series of vertices, in which edges and vertices may repeat. It is often denoted by it's vertex sequence (v1, v2, v3, ..., vn). A trail is a walk with no duplicate edges, while a path is a trail with no duplicate vertices. Sometimes, path's are given the same definition as a trail (walk w/o duplicate edges, but w/ duplicate vertices). In such a case, a path with no duplicate vertices is referred to as a simple path. The length of walk, trail, or path is the number of edges in the walk. They may be directed if their edges are directed (have direction). If the walk, trail, or path has no starting nor ending vertex (..., vn-1, vn, vn+1, ...), then it is infinite. If the walk has no start vertex but has an end vertex (v1, v2, v3, ...), then it is semi-infinite. A semi-infinite simple path is called a ray.

When the first and last vertex of a walk is distinct, then the walk is open. When they are the same, then the walk is closed. A circuit is a closed trail, while a cycle or simple circuit is a closed simple path. Similar to walks, trails, and paths, circuits may be directed if their edges are directed.

In graph theory, there are various special types of walks and circuits that are worth noting. In 1736, Leonhard Euler solved the Konigsberg Bridge problem, laying the very foundations of graph theory. At the time, Konigsberg had seven bridges connecting two islands to each other and to the mainland. The Konigsberg Bridge problem is the problem of walking through all bridges once, without repeats. Reimagined as a graph with the bridges acting as edges, the problem can be restated as whether or not there exists an Eulerian trail or Eulerian path in this graph. An Eulerian trail is a trail that visits every edge. Euler realized that such a trail only exists in a graph if there is either zero vertices of odd degree, or two vertices of odd degree. Likewise, an Eulerian circuit or Eulerian cycle is an Eulerian trail which starts and ends on the same vertex, and only exists if the graph has absolutely no vertices of odd degree.

A little over a century later, William Rowan Hamilton proposed the Icosian game. The object of the game is to find a circuit that visits every vertex of a dodecahedron. When the dodecahedron is flattened out into a two-dimensional graph, the problem can be restated as that of finding a Hamiltonian circuit or Hamiltonian cycle in this graph. A Hamiltonian circuit is a circuit which touches every vertex in the graph. When the start and end vertices are distinct, the path is called a Hamiltonian path or a traceable path. No theorem exists to prove the existence of a Hamiltonian path or circuit in a generic graph, though some theorems exists to answer this question under specific conditions. These theorems are not discussed here and is left for the reader to research further.

Connectivity

As mentioned earlier, a graph with directed edges is known as a a directed graph, or digraph, otherwise it is an undirected graph. An undirected graph is connected if there exists a path from every vertex to all other vertices in the graph. For directed graphs, if there is a directed path between every vertex in both directions, the directed graph is strongly connected. If a directed graph is connected when direction is ignored (as if it was an undirected graph), then it is weakly connected. Note that a strongly connected graph is weakly connected as well. In contrast to connect graphs, a disconnected is one where at least two vertices are not connected by any edges.

A graph is dense if the number of edges is close to maximum, usually of quadratic order |E| ∈ O( |V|2 ). A dense graph is complete if every vertex is adjacent to every other vertex in the graph. In a complete graph, |E| = |V| * (|V|-1) / 2. In contrast, a graph is sparse if the number of edges is close to minimum, usually of linear order |E| ∈ O( |V| ). Sparse graphs may be disconnected.

Cuts, Partitions, and Matchings

Graphs may be split up into disjoint sets of vertices. This partitioning is called a cut and is denoted as cut C = (S, T). When cutting a graph, any edges that connect the disjoin partitions is said to be crossing the cut, and is a member of the cut-set. Some graphs may be split up into two sets of vertices, such that in each set, no two vertices are adjacent. This cut or partition can be denoted as cut C = (S,T) such that S and T are disjoint subsets of a graph's vertex set. , and the resultant graph is called a bipartite graph (as it can be partitioned into two disjoint sets of vertices).
